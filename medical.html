Splinting

Splints Explained

A splint is a device used to hold extremities stable and rigid to prevent pain and further injuries.

Indications for Splint Use

  • Fractured long bone
  • Dislocation
  • Limited range of motion
  • Pain in extremities or joints

Purpose of a Splint

  • Decrease a patient’s pain
  • Prevent further injuries

Applying a Splint

  • For long bones: Immobilize joints above and below
  • For joints: Immobilize long bones above and below
  • Position the injured area in a comfortable position
  • Don't attempt to reposition the patient
  • Always check CMS before and after splinting

CMS Check (Circulation, Motor, Sensation)

  • Circulation: Check pulse, blood pressure, capillary refill
  • Motor: Ask patient to move parts of their body
  • Sensation: Lightly touch the area for feedback

SAM Splints

  • Form length/shape using opposite limb or your own
  • Have victim assist if able
  • Secure with bandages, distal to proximal

Bandages

  • Support, compress, and secure the splint
  • Leave a 6-inch tail to apply tension
  • Switch hands at the midline of the body
  • Overlap wraps by half, perpendicular to the body part

Cervical Collar (C-Collar)

Cervical Collars Explained

A cervical collar stabilizes the victim’s neck, which is lightweight and adjustable.

Indications for Cervical Collar Use

  • Suspected injury to the spinal cord
  • Severe fall or accident
  • Pain, loss, or decreased ability to move

Purpose of a Cervical Collar

  • Stabilizes the neck
  • Very accessible compared to other devices

Applying a Cervical Collar

  • Flip out the chin piece
  • Measure finger widths between the chin and shoulder and adjust accordingly
  • One person carefully lifts the victim’s head high
  • The second person slides the c-collar beneath the head, centering it with the spine
  • Wrap the front portion down and around the chin
  • Secure in place with the velcro strap

Spine Board

Spine Boards Explained

A spine board is a flat, rigid piece of plastic on which a body is placed and carried by a group of 4-5 people. The board secures the victim with straps and a head immobilizer to prevent further injuries.

Indicators for Spine Board Use

  • The patient must be moved to the hospital or off the scene of an incident
  • Too dangerous for the patient to stand or be moved by hand

Purpose of a Spine Board

  • To prevent further damage to the patient’s spinal cord or neck

Procedure for Spine Board Use

  • Log roll to place the patient onto the spine board safely
  • Roles
    • 1st Person: in charge of handling the patient, holding the victim’s head
    • 2nd + 3rd Person: move the patient’s body
      • One at the shoulder
      • Another at the hips
      • An optional 4th person at the legs
    • Turn together onto the patient’s side (90 degrees)
    • Wedge the board against the patient (45 degrees)
    • Log roll back onto the board and adjust accordingly

Deep Tendon Reflex

Deep Tendon Reflex Explained

The deep tendon reflex test detects neurological abnormalities involving L3-L4 spinal segments. Involves an involuntary quadriceps reflex when the hammer hits the patella tendon. Only the spinal cord is involved without any processing in the brain, testing the protective mechanism. Sensory neurons send signals to the spinal cord, which sends signals through motor neurons to jerk the knee.

Settings for Deep Tendon Reflex Testing

  • Routine physical examinations
  • Comprehensive neurological assessments
  • Test for potential nerve or muscle disorders

Purpose of a Deep Tendon Reflex Test

  • Provides insights into the patient’s nervous system
  • Tests the neurological condition
  • Identify issues with the spinal cord, nerve roots, and muscle strength and tone.
  • Helps in diagnosing conditions

Procedure for a Deep Tendon Reflex

  • The patient sits at the end of the exam table with their legs dangling freely.
  • Distract the patient via conversations and/or closing their eyes to prevent anticipation and tension.
  • Sit beside the patient and locate the patellar tendon
  • Strike the patellar tendon with the reflex hammer
  • Observe the response of the reflex
  • Repeat 5-6 times
  • Switch to the other knee

Normal Response

  • Quick and brisk extension of the lower leg
  • No delay between tap and response
  • Symmetrical between both legs

Abnormal Response

  • Hyperreflexia: exaggerated reflex response
  • Hyporeflexia: diminished or absent reflex response
  • Asymmetry: different responses between knees
  • Clonus: rhythmic, involuntary muscle contractions

Snellen Eye Chart

Snellen Eye Chart Explained

The snellen eye chart tests the cranial nerve II and evaluates the patient’s vision.

Settings for Snellen Eye Chart Use

  • During routine check-ups
  • Pre-employment or driving license tests in which vision standards are required
  • Monitor vision in patients with known eye conditions

Purpose of a Snellen Eye Chart

  • To test visual acuity

Procedure for a Snellen Eye Chart

  • Place the patient 20 feet away from the chart
  • Ask the patient if they wear glasses or contacts (If so, instruct the patient to wear during exam)
  • Ask which line number they can read
  • Have the patient read aloud the row of letters (cover the left eye, then right eye, then both eyes)
  • Continue until a mistake has been made

Result Meanings

  • Corrected Vision: The patient wears glasses or contacts
  • The first number represents the distance the patient can see the chart
  • The second number indicates the distance a person with normal vision would be able to read the same line
  • Ex. 20/30 means the patient sees at 20 feet what someone with normal vision would see at 30 feet

Rinne and Weber Hearing Tests

Rinne and Weber Hearing Tests Explained

Procedure of a Rinne Hearing Test

  • Instruct the patient to signal once they can’t hear the pitch
  • Pinch or strike a 512 Hz tuning fork
  • Place the fork on the mastoid process behind one ear
  • Once the patient signals, move the tuning fork to the ear canal
  • The patient will signal once more, signalling the end of the test
  • Record the length of time between each process

Interpreting Results of a Rinne Hearing Test

  • Normal Hearing: Air conduction time is twice as long as bone conduction time
  • Conductive Hearing Loss: Bone conduction is heard longer than air conduction, indicating a problem in the outer or middle ear

Procedure of a Weber Hearing Test

  • Pinch or strike the tuning fork
  • Place the tuning fork on the top-middle of the patient’s head
  • Ask which ear the note is best heard in (left, right, or equal)

Interpreting Results of a Weber Hearing Test

  • Normal Hearing: Sound is heard equally in both ears
  • Conductive Hearing Loss: Sound is heard best in the ear with the abnormality
  • Sensorineural Hearing Loss: Sound is heard best in the normal ear

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR)

CPR Explained

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation, also known as CPR, is the use of chest compressions and rescue breaths to provide artificial respiration and circulation to a victim whose blood flow and breathing have ceased.

Procedure of Adult CPR

  1. Assess the scene and check for safety
    • Safe: approach the victim
    • Not Safe: do not approach and call 911
  2. Check for responsiveness (T&S)
    • Responsive: ask secondary assessment questions
    • Unresponsive: directly instruct a bystander to call 911 and another to fetch a First Aid kit and AED
  3. Check for breathing and pulse
    • Procedures
      • Pulse: place 2-3 fingers on the carotid artery on the side closest to the rescuer
      • Breathing: watch the rise and fall of the chest
    • Interpreting Results
      • No Pulse, No Breathing: begin CPR
      • Pulse, No Breathing: provide rescue breaths
      • Pulse, Breathing: monitor victim
  4. Perform CPR
    • place the victim on a flat, firm surface
    • bare the chest
    • CAB Procedure
      • Chest Compressions
        • Interlock hands and lock elbows
        • Place the heel of the palm on the middle of the chest
        • 'Push hard and push fast' at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute with a depth of two inches
        • Give 30 compressions
        • Minimize interruptions
      • Airway
        • Head tilt, chin lift maneuver to open the airway
      • Breaths
        • Provide two breaths
        • One second inhale, one second exhale
        • No Chest Rise: the airway wasn't opened or the patient is choking
  5. Continue cycles until the EMT's arrival or an AED is provided

Procedure of Rescue Breaths

  • Pocket Mask
    • Seal the pocket mask
      • Place the thumb and index finger along the edge of the mask
      • Two hands cupping the mask
    • Use the bridge of the nose to correct positioning
    • Hold the mask firmly against the face
    • Head tilt, chin lift maneuver and give breaths
  • Bag Mask
    • Seal by wrapping the thumb and the index finger in a ‘C’ shape around the valve
    • Shape the other fingers in an ‘E’ to lift the chin and open the airway
    • Place onto the bridge of the nose, watching the bulge
    • Squeeze the bag with the other hand or leg
  • Mouth-to-Mouth
    • Place one hand on the forehead and pinch the nose
    • Tilt the chin up using the other hand
    • Seal the mouth around the victim’s mouth and breathe for them

Automated External Defibrillator (AED)

AED Explained

An automated external defibrillator (AED) is a medical device designed to analyze the heart rhythm and deliver an electric shock to ventricular fibrillation (VF) to restore it to normal.

Procedure of Adult AED

  • Preparation
    • Once an AED is received, place it next to the victim’s head
    • Turn the AED on immediately
    • Follow automated instructions from the AED
    • Ensure the chest is bare before applying pads:
      • Upper right chest
      • Lower left chest
    • Press pads firmly against the victim’s chest, ensuring skin contact
  • Analyzing
    • Once AED acquires a connection, it will announce that it is analyzing
    • Yell ‘CLEAR!’ ensuring people step away from the victim
    • Allow the AED to process the victim’s heart rhythm
  • Results
    • No Shock Advised
      • Continue CPR until the AED analyzes once more
    • Shock Advised
      • Yell ‘Clear!’
      • Make sure everyone steps away from the body
      • An automatic countdown or a manual shock via button press will deliver the electric shock
  • Special Cases
    • Hairy Chest: shave the body with a razor, wax, hand, or use an extra pad to rip chest hair off
    • Implant: avoid placing pads directly onto implants; place pads an inch away
    • Metal Clothing: avoid contact
    • Water or Liquid: move the victim to a dry area and dry the chest as much and as fast as possible

Two Rescuer CPR/AED

Two-Rescuer CPR/AED Explained

Two-rescuer CPR/AED ensures maximum effectiveness of compressions, breaths, and AED use as one rescuer manages compressions while the other rescuer provides breaths and manages the AED.

Procedure of Two Rescuer CPR/AED

  • First Rescuer: Compressions
    • Usually the first on the scene and already performing CPR for the victim
    • CPR procedure (30 compressions, 2 breaths)
  • Second Rescuer: AED and Breaths
    • FIRST RESCUER DOES NOT STOP CPR
    • Bring an AED over and attach pads to the victim
    • Responsible for giving breaths, preferably through a bag mask, kneeling at the victim’s head
    • Provides compression feedback for the first rescuer
  • Analysis
    • Once the AED announces analysis, the rescuer handling the AED clears people away from the body
    • The first rescuer stops CPR and steps away
    • If a shock is advised, the AED rescuer clears again before pressing the shock button
  • Switch tasks after 5 cycles of compressions and breaths (usually 2 minutes or when the AED analyzes again) or if the rescuer giving compressions tires

Vital Signs: Temperature

Vital Signs Explained

Vital signs provide information about a patient’s basic body conditions. There are five major vital signs: temperature, pulse, respirations, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation. Each is crucial to maintaining homeostasis within the human body because if any of them are abnormal, the delicate state of balance is thrown into disarray.

Temperature

The balance between heat lost (perspiration, respiration, excretion) and produced by the body (food metabolism, muscle and gland activity) is usually measured in Fahrenheit but occasionally in Celsius.

  • Oral
    • Taken in the mouth using an electronic or clinical thermometer
    • The patient shouldn’t eat, drink, or smoke 15 minutes before taking their temperature
    • Procedure:
      • Take the probe off the charging unit
      • Ensure the oral mode is displayed and insert a disposable cover (ejection button will pop out)
      • Place the probe in the posterior sublingual pocket and have the patient hold the probe with their lips
      • Have the patient breathe through their nose as the temperature is taken
      • A beep sound will announce the completion of taking the temperature, and it will be displayed on the probe
      • Tell the patient their temperature and discard the cover
      • Place the probe inside the well to reset or turn off
    • Ex. T(O) = 98.4°F
  • Aural
    • Taken in the auditory canal using a tympanic thermometer
    • Measures thermal, infrared energy radiating from blood vessels in the tympanic membrane
    • Similar to taking a rectal temperature
    • Fast and convenient method
    • Can be inaccurate if inserted incorrectly or if the ear is infected/contains excessive earwax
    • Procedure:
      • Remove from the cradle by gripping at the base and lifting upward
      • Attach the cover to the probe at a 90-degree angle
      • Once the ring light turns green, three dashed lines will indicate readiness
      • Place the probe inside the ear canal, directly toward the opposite temple
      • Keep the thermometer steady and press start
      • A long beep and green light indicate the temperature has been taken.
      • Read and record the temperature shown on the display
      • Remove the cover by pressing the eject button, and put it back into the cradle
    • Ex. T(A) = 96.5°F
  • Temporal
    • Taken on the forehead using a temporal scanning thermometer
    • Measures temperature in the temporal artery to provide an accurate reading
    • Procedure:
      • Remove the protective cap
      • Place on the center of the forehead
      • Press and hold while scanning across the forehead
      • Slide across the hairline and end by pressing against the mastoid process
      • Release and read the temperature
    • Ex. T(TA) = 98.4°F
  • Results
    • Normal
      • 97 - 100 degrees Fahrenheit or 36.1 - 37.8 degrees Celsius
      • Reasons for varying temperatures:
        • Individual differences between humans
        • Time of day (lower in morning, higher in evening)
        • Location
    • Abnormal
      • Hypothermia: body temperature is below 95 F
      • Pyrexia (Fever): body temperature is above 101 F
      • Hyperthermia: rectal body temperature exceeds 104 F

Vital Signs: Pulse

Pulse Explained

Blood pressure pushes against an artery wall as the heart contracts and relaxes. It is usually felt in the arteries closest to the skin and can be pressed against the bone with the fingers. Pulse takes in multiple factors, such as rate (the number of beats per minute), rhythm (the regularity of the pulse), and volume (strength, force, quality, or intensity).

  • Procedure:
    • Place two to three fingers on an artery to take the pulse
      • Conscious: radial artery or any superficial artery with a bone behind
      • Unconscious: carotid artery in the neck
    • Start counting with ‘0’ for 30 seconds
    • Multiply the number by two to calculate the bpm
    • Take note of the rhythm and force with which the pulse beats
    • Ex. P55, regular, weak
  • Results
    • Normal
      • Pulse Rate: 60 - 100 bpm
      • Pulse Rhythm: consistent spacing of beats
      • Pulse Volume: normal
    • Abnormal
      • Bradycardia: pulse is under 60 bpm
      • Tachycardia: pulse is above 100 bpm
      • Arrhythmia: irregular or abnormal pattern usually caused by a defect in the electrical conduction pattern of the heart (count one full minute)
      • For pulse volume, it could be described as bounding, strong, faint, weak, or thready

Vital Signs: Respirations

Respirations Explained

Respiration is the process of taking in oxygen and expelling carbon dioxide from the lungs and the respiratory tract. Respirations also take into account the rhythm, depth, and quality of breaths.

  • Procedure:
    • After taking a patient’s pulse, the respirations must be taken in a way to keep the patient unaware
    • Leave your hand on the pulse site while counting respirations
    • An aware patient can control their respiratory system, causing inaccurate results
    • Ex. 15 brpm
  • Results
    • Normal
      • measured in breaths per minute (bpm)
      • Adults: 12 - 20
      • Children: 16 - 30
      • Infants: 30 - 50
    • Abnormal
      • Dyspnea: difficulty breathing
      • Tachypnea: rapid, shallow breathing
      • Bradypnea: slow rate of breathing
      • Orthopnea: severe dyspnea in which breathing is difficult in any position other than sitting and standing
      • Cheyne-Stokes: abnormal breathing pattern characterized by periods of dyspnea followed by periods of apnea (frequent in dying patients)
      • Rales: bubbling, crackling, or noisy sounds caused by fluids or mucus
      • Wheezing: difficulty breathing with high-pitched whistling sounds during expiration caused by narrow passages
      • Cyanosis: bluish discoloration of the skin, lips, and/or nail beds due to decreased oxygen

Vital Signs: Oxgyen Saturation

Oxgyen Saturation Explained

Oxygen saturation is a non-invasive method to measure oxygen saturation in the blood. This method uses light absorption through the fingertip or earlobe, providing real-time measurements of oxygen saturation percentage as well as heart rate. Using two types of light, red and infrared light, the light passes through blood vessels and measures the difference in light absorption between oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin. Results will show SpO2 percentage and pulse rate (bpm).

  • Procedure:
    • Ensure the sensor is clean before beginning and that the patient has no nail polish and/or artificial nails.
    • Lightly feel the patient’s fingers for warmth.
    • Place the device correctly on the fingertip, keeping the hand still during the reading
    • Wait for steady reading and then record
    • SpO2: 98% Heart Rate: 52 BPM
  • Results
    • Normal
      • Range: 95% - 100% SpO2
      • Resting Heart Rate: 60 - 100 bpm
    • Abnormal
      • Mild Hypoxia: 90% - 94% SpO2
      • to Severe Hypoxia: less than 90% SpO2

Vital Signs: Blood Pressure

Blood Pressure Explained

Blood pressure measures the pressure blood exerts on the walls of arteries during various stages of heart activity. You can read blood pressure in mmHg electronically or manually using a sphygmomanometer. Systolic pressure is the pressure in the walls of the arteries when the left ventricle contracts, and diastolic pressure is the constant pressure in the walls of the arteries when the left ventricle is at rest.

  • Procedure
    • Squeeze the air out of the sphygmomanometer and tighten the pump
    • Apply to the patient’s arm (above the elbow) and rotate correctly so the arrow is on the brachial artery
    • Velcro and clip the dial onto the sphygmomanometer
    • Prepare the stethoscope by rotating the end piece 180 degrees to test if the correct side is on by tapping
    • Place the stethoscope over the brachial artery and hold the pump in the other hand with the fingers on the knob
    • Rapidly pump air into the sphygmonomanometer until the dial reads 160 mm/Hg
    • Slowly release air at a consistent rate by opening the knob
    • Systolic pressure is the first tap you hear, and diastolic pressure is when the taps end
    • Unscrew knob to release the air and take off the sphygmomanometer
    • Make sure to not keep the sphygmomanometer on the patient for any longer than needed, as it can stop circulation
    • Ex. 102/62, right arm
  • Results
    • Normal Results
      • Systolic Pressure: between 90 - 120
      • Diastolic Pressure: between 60 - 80
      • Normal BP: 120/80 (systolic/diastolic)
    • Abnormal Results
      • Hypertension (high blood pressure): greater than 140/90, “silent killer”
      • Hypotension (low blood pressure): less than 90/60
      • Orthostatic Hypotension: sudden drop in systolic and diastolic pressure when someone moves suddenly

Stop the Bleed

ABCs of Primary Assessment

  • A - Airway
  • B - Breathing
  • C - Circulation
  • S - Severe Bleeding

ABCs of Bleeding Control

  • A - Alert 911
    • Call 911
    • Calmly describe the situation (when, where, who)
    • Follow the instructions provided by the 911 operator
  • B - Bleeding
    • Find the source of bleeding
    • Check for continuous bleeding, large volume of bleeding, or pooling
    • Clothing may hide bleeding
    • The victim may be bleeding in multiple places
    • Check extremities, armpits, neck, groin, and body
  • C - Compression
    • Direct Pressure
      • Use sterile gauze or any absorbent material
      • Place at the location of bleeding
      • Maintain pressure until help arrives
    • Packing
      • If bleeding continues and if the wound is deep enough, pack it
      • Utilize roller gauze, preferably
      • Pack roller gauze into the wound until it is filled, and apply pressure
      • Maintain pressure until help arrives
    • Tourniquet
      • Apply 2-3 inches proximal to the wound
      • o NOT place over the elbow or the knee
      • Tighten the tourniquet until bleeding stops
      • A second tourniquet may be required
      • Write down the time the tourniquet was applied
      • DO NOT REMOVE

Epinephrine Pen (EpiPen®)

Epinephrine Explained

A medical device that is used to quickly inject a dose of epinephrine during a severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis). Designed for easy, emergency uses and often carried by individuals with severe allergic reactions.

Components

  • Safety cap (blue)
  • Auto-injector housing
  • Needle end (orange)
  • Dose indicator window
  • Built-in needle protection system
  • Precautions
    • Check expiration date
    • Inspect solution clarity
    • Store at room temperature

Uses of an EpiPen®

  • Acts as an alpha/beta adrenergic agonist that opens airways, improves BP, and reduces swelling
  • s/s: swelling, weezing, itchy lumps or hives, nausea, hoarse voice
  • Can quickly reverse the life-threatening symptoms of anaphylactic shock across multiple body systems
  • Delays can lead to worsening symptoms
  • After administering, physiologic changes include increased BP, stabilized heart function, and reduced swelling
  • Procedure
    • Remove from the carrier tube and grasp with the needle downward
    • Pull the blue straight out
    • Swing into the patient (click means the injection is given)
    • Wait 3 seconds before moving and massage the area for 10 seconds
    • Use another needle if needed
  • After-Care
    • Record administration time
    • Call 911 and monitor vital signs
    • Position the patient properly and prepare for EMS arrival
    • Give the used autoinjector to EMS for proper disposal

Shoulder Sling

Shoulder Slings Explained

A medical device that uses fabric or straps to support and immobilize the arm, elbow, and shoulder after injury. This sling can utilize a triangular bandage, straps, fasteners, pillows, and padding materials. Different types of shoulder slings include triangular bandage, simple arm sling, immobilizer swathe, pneumatic sling, figure-8 brace, and abduction sling.

Indicators to Utilize a Shoulder Sling

  • Clavicle fractures
  • Rotator cuff repairs
  • AC joint injuries
  • Upper extremity trauma
  • Duration: usually 2 to 6 weeks, worn continuously

Uses of a Shoulder Sling

  • Used to immobilize the shoulder to help the body heal after an injury
  • Helps reduce pain, limit movement, and allow the tissues to heal properly

Applying a Shoulder Sling

  • Fitting and Adjustment
    • Elbow at 90 degrees with the wrist at or above the elbow
    • Even weight distribution
    • Comfortable knot/strap placement
  • Safety
    • Watch for skin pressure points
    • Monitor circulation
    • Maintain proper positioning
